Context

Approximately one third of patients with focal epilepsy is therapy-refractory, and deserves presurgical epilepsy evaluation to identify suitable surgical candidates. Invasive intracranial EEG recording is an established method in presurgical epilepsy evaluation. It is an excellent tool to localize seizure generators and to study brain regions with abnormal activity. In contrast to the comprehensive literature on physiological variations in the scalp EEG of healthy individuals, the knowledge accumulated on normal intracranial cortical activity in the human brain is sparse and based mostly on the clinical experience gathered at the different expert centers performing intracranial depth EEG recordings in patients with epilepsy.

The main reasons for the lack of normative data in the different brain regions are

  • The difficulty of standardization of the electrode placement compared to scalp EEG resulting in problems to perform inter-individual comparisons of EEG oscillations
  • The relatively rare placement of electrodes in healthy brain tissue and the difficulty in identifying healthy brain

Our main goal is to develop a 3-D brain atlas of normal electrophysiological brain activity.

The development of such an atlas will provide further insights into the topographical characterization of physiological EEG oscillations, which is important for the understanding of pathological as opposed to physiological EEG activity. We will analyze intracranial EEG data from what was retrospectively identified to be normal brain regions, and examine topographical differences according to different brain sites and regions. We hypothesize that the EEG activity differs across different cortical regions. These differences are important to consider when interpreting the intracerebral EEG during presurgical epilepsy work-up.

This project can only be achieved in a multicenter collaboration, as data from healthy brain regions from many patients are necessary to overcome the limited sampling of healthy brain with intracerebral electrodes. Furthermore, in order to merge data from multiple subjects, intracerebral electrode positions need to be standardized in a common 3D environment using a stereotactic template providing the exact coordinates of each recording contact. The existence of such an atlas of normal brain activity will aid to improve the challenging differentiation between physiological and pathological brain activity, resulting in improved presurgical epilepsy work-up.
